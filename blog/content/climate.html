Climate Change Explained

Understanding Global Warming, Its Causes, Impacts, and Solutions

The Science of a Warming Planet

What is Climate Change?

Climate change refers to long-term shifts in temperatures and weather patterns. While these shifts can be natural, since the 1800s, human activities have been the main driver of climate change, primarily due to the burning of fossil fuels like coal, oil, and gas, which produces heat-trapping greenhouse gases.

The Earth's climate has changed throughout history. However, the current warming trend is of particular significance because it is proceeding at a rate that is unprecedented in the history of modern civilization and is extremely likely (greater than 95% probability) to be the result of human activity since the mid-20th century.

The Greenhouse Effect

The greenhouse effect is a natural process that warms the Earth's surface. When the Sun's energy reaches the Earth's atmosphere, some of it is reflected back to space and the rest is absorbed and re-radiated by greenhouse gases. Greenhouse gases include water vapor, carbon dioxide, methane, nitrous oxide, and ozone. Human activities are increasing the concentrations of these gases, enhancing the greenhouse effect and causing global warming.

Key Climate Data

The Earth's average surface temperature has risen about 1.1°C since the late 19th century, with most of the warming occurring in the past 40 years. The years 2016 and 2020 are tied for the warmest years on record.

Primary Causes of Climate Change

  • Burning Fossil Fuels: Coal, oil, and gas combustion for energy and transportation accounts for over 75% of global greenhouse gas emissions.
  • Deforestation: Trees absorb CO₂, so cutting them down not only releases stored carbon but reduces the planet's capacity to absorb emissions.
  • Agriculture: Livestock production, rice cultivation, and fertilizer use generate significant methane and nitrous oxide emissions.
  • Industrial Processes: Cement production, chemical manufacturing, and other industrial activities release various greenhouse gases.
  • Waste Management: Landfills produce methane as organic waste decomposes without oxygen.

Current Status

Atmospheric CO₂ levels have reached 420 parts per million, the highest in at least 800,000 years. The last decade was the warmest on record, and each of the last four decades has been warmer than any decade since 1850.

Observed Impacts of Climate Change

Rising Temperatures

Global average temperature increase of 1.1°C since pre-industrial times.

Sea Level Rise

Global sea level rose about 20 cm in the last century, accelerating in recent decades.

Extreme Weather

Increased frequency and intensity of heatwaves, droughts, floods, and storms.

Melting Ice

Arctic sea ice declining at a rate of about 13% per decade; glaciers retreating worldwide.

Key Climate Terms

Global Warming

The long-term heating of Earth's climate system observed since the pre-industrial period due to human activities.

Greenhouse Gases

Gases that trap heat in the atmosphere, including carbon dioxide, methane, nitrous oxide, and fluorinated gases.

Carbon Footprint

The total amount of greenhouse gases produced directly and indirectly by human activities.

Climate Mitigation

Efforts to reduce or prevent emission of greenhouse gases to limit the magnitude of future warming.

Climate Adaptation

Adjusting to actual or expected climate change and its effects to moderate harm or exploit beneficial opportunities.

International Climate Agreements

1992 UN Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) established at Earth Summit in Rio.
1997 Kyoto Protocol adopted, binding developed countries to emission reduction targets.
2015 Paris Agreement adopted by 196 parties, aiming to limit global warming to well below 2°C.
2021 COP26 in Glasgow strengthened commitments with Glasgow Climate Pact.

Solutions and Actions

Addressing climate change requires both mitigation (reducing emissions) and adaptation (preparing for impacts). Here are key solutions:

Renewable Energy Transition

Shifting from fossil fuels to solar, wind, hydro, and geothermal energy. Solar and wind are now often cheaper than fossil fuels.

Energy Efficiency

Improving efficiency in buildings, transportation, and industry could reduce global energy use by one-third.

Sustainable Transportation

Electric vehicles, public transit, biking, and walking reduce transportation emissions.

Forest Conservation

Protecting and restoring forests, which absorb about 2.6 billion tonnes of CO₂ each year.

Sustainable Agriculture

Practices that reduce emissions, improve soil health, and increase carbon sequestration.

Individual Actions That Make a Difference

Reduce Energy Use: Use energy-efficient appliances, LED lighting, and properly insulate your home.

Transportation Choices: Use public transit, carpool, bike, walk, or switch to electric vehicles.

Diet Changes: Reduce meat consumption, especially beef, and choose locally produced foods.

Waste Reduction: Reduce, reuse, recycle, and compost organic waste.

Advocate: Support climate policies and businesses with sustainable practices.

The Path Forward

According to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), limiting global warming to 1.5°C requires rapid, far-reaching, and unprecedented changes in all aspects of society. While the challenge is immense, the technology and knowledge to address it exist. What's needed is political will, economic investment, and collective action at all levels—from individuals to international organizations.

Major Greenhouse Gases

Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)

76% of emissions; from fossil fuels and deforestation.

Methane (CH₄)

16% of emissions; from agriculture, waste, fossil fuels.

Nitrous Oxide (N₂O)

6% of emissions; from agriculture and industry.

Fluorinated Gases

2% of emissions; synthetic, high global warming potential.

Climate Projections

Without additional mitigation efforts beyond those in place today, global temperatures are projected to increase by 3.7°C to 4.8°C by 2100 compared to pre-industrial levels.
